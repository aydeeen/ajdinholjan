It is easy for front-end developers to get snobby about WordPress.

I understand why. We have all seen messy themes, plugin overload, fragile page builders, and admin screens that feel like a storage room nobody has cleaned in years. But dismissing WordPress completely is lazy thinking.

Sometimes WordPress is exactly the right tool.

If the client needs content control, familiar publishing workflows, media management, roles, revisions, SEO plugins, forms, and a budget that does not justify a custom CMS, WordPress can be a very practical choice. The job is not to force every project into the newest stack. The job is to choose the stack that fits the reality.

## The modern way to think about it

I try to separate WordPress as a content platform from WordPress as a front-end experience.

You can build a careful custom theme. You can use blocks responsibly. You can pair WordPress with a modern front-end when that makes sense. You can keep plugins limited, audit performance, and make the editor experience pleasant for the people who will actually maintain the site.

The bad version of WordPress is not inevitable. It is usually the result of unmanaged decisions.

The good version is boring in the best way: editors can publish, developers can reason about the theme, performance stays acceptable, and the business can keep moving without opening a ticket for every text change.

That is not glamorous, but it is useful.

And useful is still a very good reason to pick a tool.
